Though it took 17 years, research continued on the Edison effect. The element that gave off light in a light bulb was re-designed so that it no longer gave off light but merely gave off electrons. It was called the cathode. The extra element that was added by Edisonâ€™s scientist was permanently added to the tube and called the anode. The anode is also often called the plate. Since there were now two elements, this became known as a diode, "di" meaning two.

When the Edison Effect happens, electrons flow through the tube from cathode to anode as shown in Figure 2-1. Remember that current flow (I) and electron flow (e) are the same, they are only opposite in direction. For current to flow through the tube, two conditions have to be met:

  1. The cathode must be heated.

  2. The anode must be charged more positively than the cathode.

When the Anode is more positive than the cathode or the cathode is more negative than the anode it is said that the tube is correctly biased.

Diodes are either directly heated or indirectly heated. With a directly heated cathode, a current is passed through the cathode in order to heat it. With an indirectly heated cathode, a separate heater is wrapped around the cathode in order to produce heat. The schematic symbols for directly and indirectly heated diodes are shown in Figure 2-2. The circle that surrounds the tube elements represents the vacuum envelope of the tube. This is commonly made of glass or metal.

When both of the conditions needed for current flow are met (heated cathode and anode more positive than cathode) then electrons will flow from the heated cathode to the anode. This means that current is flowing from the Anode to the Cathode.
