There are two extremes of current flow in a vacuum tube. When no more current can flow through the tube, no matter how much more positive the control grid is made, the tube is said to be in saturation. When no less current can be made to flow, no matter how much more negative the control grid is made, the tube is said to be in cutoff. Figure 10-1 shows a graph of cutoff and saturation.

  • Saturation: Maximum current flow through tube.

  • Cutoff: Minimum current flow through tube.

Most audio waveforms are symmetrical, or at least average out to be. That means an audio amplifier must allow for the same amount of positive peak as negative peak. A positive voltage input increases current while a negative voltage input decreases current. To allow the same amount of room for the positive and negative peaks, we need to be able to increase current as much as we can decrease current. We can increase current to the maximum of saturation and decrease current to the minimum of cutoff. Those are the extremes of current within the tube.

Balancing Saturation and Cutoff

To allow room to both increase current (up to saturation) and decrease current (down to cutoff) the current needs to be halfway between saturation and cutoff when there is 0V on the grid.

That means that a bit over half of the maximum current that will ever flow is flowing when no audio is being amplified! This type of amplifier has current flowing all of the time and is called a class A amplifier. While they are not very efficient, they are sought after for their audio quality.
