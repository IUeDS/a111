It turns out that when the cathode connected to ground, 0V at the control grid places current through the tube at slightly closer to saturation than it is to cutoff. This means that the control grid is too positive compared to the cathode. There are two ways to fix this problem so that 0V at the control grid sets current half way between saturation and cutoff:

  1. We could add a negative DC voltage to the AC on the control grid.
    This is sometimes done with other tube types, that we will discuss later, when used in high power situations. It is not typical, however, for most triode circuits. This is because it requires a separate negative DC voltage, which would not be needed otherwise. This negative DC voltage would be called the bias voltage. A circuit utilizing this would be said to have an external bias.
  2. Figure 11-1 shows the other method of solving this, called self-bias or cathode-bias. Instead of making the control grid more negative, this method makes the cathode more positive. This is easily done with a resistor connected between ground and the cathode. This resistor drops voltage moving the cathode more positive than ground. The electrons moving from the cathode only know the voltage difference between where they started (the cathode) and the control grid, so making the cathode more positive has the same effect as making the control grid more negative. It is, however, much easier.

We have a problem

Adding a resistor (R2 in figure 11-1) allows 0V on the control grid to produce current through the tube that is halfway between saturation and cutoff. However, when the control grid voltage is changed (say with audio) the voltage drop of R2 also changes. This causes the cathode voltage to change along with the control grid voltage. Current flow through the tube is controlled by the difference between the cathode and the control grid. If the cathode becomes more positive at the same time that the control grid is becoming more positive the difference between the two does not change as desired and we end up with a smaller AC voltage at the output.

A Solution

The signal flowing between ground and the cathode is all moving one direction but it varies in voltage in response to the voltage at the control grid. If the signal on the control grid is AC, as it would be for audio, then the signal flowing from ground to the cathode would be AC + DC.

  • The DC portion sets current (with 0V at control grid) halfway between saturation and cutoff.
  • The AC portion creates a varying voltage drop across R2 that changes the cathode voltage and reduces output voltage.

What we need is a way to separate the AC and DC portions of the signal. Hopefully you remember that we investigated such a circuit in the Chapter 4 investigation. The circuit in the Chapter 4 investigation utilized a resistor and capacitor in parallel to give separate paths for AC and DC. We can utilize that here as well.
If we place a capacitor in parallel with R2 (C3 in figure 5-16) that will become a low reactance path for AC while the DC will have to flow through R2.

  • AC will flow through AC as it is the easier path:
    • Low reactance means a low voltage drop for AC.
    • Low voltage drop for AC means that the cathode voltage will not change with control grid voltage changes.
  • DC will flow through R2 as it cannot flow through C3:
    • Voltage drop created by R2 sets cathode voltage so that 0V on control grid creates current through tube that is halfway between saturation and cutoff.

Common Cathode Component Functions

  • R1: Converts changing current through tube into a changing voltage.
  • R2: Moves cathode above ground to center current (with no Grid voltage) halfway between saturation and cutoff.
  • C1: Block DC from input so that it does not affect bias.
  • C2: Block DC from getting to/from next components.
  • C3: Provides low reactance path for AC so that amplification is not decreased by voltage change of cathode.
