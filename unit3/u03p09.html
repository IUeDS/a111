If you wind two inductors together and put AC through one, it will create a magnetic field that will be transferred to the second inductor. That magnetic field will induce an electrical current into the second inductor.

Does that seem like a lot of work to go from current to current?
Well, it turns out that if we change the number of coils of wire on the second inductor (we will call it the secondary), that will change the amount of current that the magnetic field from the first inductor (the primary) induces into the secondary. The relationship of the number of coils of the two inductors is called the turns ratio.

We have now created a transformer. Two schematic symbols for transformers are shown in Figure 9-1.

The two images in Figure 9-1 are simply different ways of drawing transformers, there is no meaning in the differences. Some real transformers are shown in Figure 9-2.

DC will pass through a transformer primary but will not induce any current into the secondary.
That means that DC will not pass from one side on a transformer to the other, only AC will.

Turns Ratio and Voltage

The law of conservation of energy in physics tells us that we canâ€™t simply create more current. Instead we have to convert it from something else. In the case of a transformer, we are exchanging voltage for current. If we want more current on the secondary than we put into the primary, we will need more voltage on the primary than we will get on the secondary.

The power of the secondary $(V\times I)$ must equal the power of the primary $(V\times I)$.

The turns ratio is also the ratio of voltage from primary to secondary. A turns ratio of 10:1, for instance, would mean that $10V_{RMS}$ on the primary would produce $1V_{RMS}$ on the secondary. This is shown in Figure 9-3.

Current has an inverse relationship to turns ratio. In the example above the 10:1 turns ratio with 1A on the primary would mean $10A$ on the secondary.

When current flows into the primary of a transformer going from top to bottom (clockwise) it induces a current in the secondary that goes from bottom to top. This current (from bottom to top in the secondary) creates a current in the circuit that is connected to the secondary that flows clockwise.

This is indicated in some schematic symbols with a:

  • + on the primary top and secondary bottom

  • a dot instead of the +

  • sometimes it is not indicated at all

In all those cases, though, current flowing from top to bottom on the primary creates a current that flows from bottom to top in the secondary. This is shown in the interactive example to the left, with red indicating electron flow for the positive half of the waveform on the primary and blue indicating electron flow for the negative half.

Center-tapped Transformer

Figure 9-4 shows a center-tapped transformer. The center-tap is a connection half way along the "inductor" that creates the secondary. This splits the voltage of the secondary. The turns ratio of the transformer describes the turns of the total secondary, so each half would have half as many turns.

Uses for Transformers

Transformers are found all over in the modern world. Here are some of the common uses for transformers:

  • Voltage conversion: Electricity is delivered to homes and businesses at $120V_{RMS}$ or $220V_{RMS}$ but very little equipment (like computers, stereos, TVs) actually use those voltages. Transformers are used to convert to a different (usually lower) voltage.

  • Electrical isolation: Audio circuits often use transformers for electrical isolation because only a magnetic field connects the primary and secondary.

  • Balanced Lines: Transformers are also the easiest (though not cheapest) way to create balanced lines. More on balanced lines will be coming later.

  • Impedance conversion: Transformers are also used for impedance conversion. For instance, if you have ever used a direct box (DI) you have used a transformer for impedance conversion. A direct box converts the impedance coming from an instrument which is a very high impedance (such as an electric bass guitar) into a lower impedance to match what a microphone preamp is expecting.

Impedance is the "effective resistance" to AC current flow. This is the combination of resistance and reactance. Input and output impedances will be discussed in later chapters.
