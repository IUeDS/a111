Flat

Figure 4-1 shows the schematic of an AC signal source connected first through an inductor and then into a speaker. The speaker schematic symbol is on the far right of the schematic and is the load for this circuit. Speakers are electrical devices that convert electrical current into sound.

If we place an inductor in series between an audio source and the load, as shown in Figure 4-1, we create a low pass filter. Low pass filters are also known as high cut filters. They pass the low frequencies and cut the high frequencies. The low pass filter in Figure 4-1, as well as all the filters we will be discussing in chapters 3 and 4 are all passive filters. Passive filters can reduce voltage, but not increase it. In later chapters we will discuss active filters.

With an inductor, the reactance is higher for higher frequencies and lower for lower frequencies. In other words, low frequencies get through easier than high frequencies. For these examples we will use a speaker as the load in our schematics.

To represent frequency response, we will use a graph that has a vertical (y) axis that represents output voltage (or volume). The horizontal (x) axis represents frequency from low (left) to high (right). This graph is referred to as the frequency response curve.

Figure 4-1 shows the frequency response drawn for the low pass filter created by an inductor in series as well as the schematic.

This is just a very simple representation of the frequency response of the filter, more detailed representations that will include numeric frequency values will come later in the chapter.

Inductance Increased
Original Low Pass

If we increase the value of the inductance, either by adding more inductors in series or swapping for a larger inductor, the graph for frequency response will move to the left as shown in Figure 4-2. A simple way to remember this is that toward the left of the graph are the lower frequencies which are larger in wavelength.

When Inductance Changes
Inductance value increased = curve moves to the left.
Inductance value decreased = curve moves to the right.

Larger inductance = Lower Frequencies.

Inductance Decreased
Original Low Pass

If we decrease the value of the inductance, either by adding inductors in parallel with the first inductor, or using a smaller inductor, the graph for the frequency response will move to the right as shown in Figure 4-3. Again the higher frequencies have a smaller wavelength so making the inductance smaller will move the curve to the right, toward the smaller (higher) frequencies.

This idea that the frequency response curve will move to the left for an increase in value (of inductance in this case), or to the right for a decrease in value, ends up being very useful. It works for Inductors in series or parallel and it also works for capacitors which we will discuss in Chapter 4.

Basic Inductor Rules

1) Inductors allow lower frequencies to pass easier than high frequencies.
or The higher the frequency, the larger the reactance ($X_L$ ) of an inductor. (larger $X_L$ = larger $V$ drop)

2) The larger the inductance/the larger the wavelength (lower the Frequency) that will be passed.
