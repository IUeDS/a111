If we place an inductor in parallel with the load fed by an audio source, as shown in Figure 5-1, we create a high pass filter. This means that high frequencies get through to the output easier than low frequencies. In the circuit shown the load is represented by a speaker. Figure 5-1 shows the frequency response drawn for the high pass filter created by an inductor in parallel. The inductor itself still does the same thing. It has a higher reactance for high frequencies. Ohmâ€™s law (which does work for reactance) tells us that a higher reactance means a higher voltage drop.

When the inductor was connected in series, that higher voltage drop for higher frequencies meant that the inductor dropped more voltage for higher frequencies, leaving less remaining to get to the load. This created the low pass filter we discussed in 3-4.

When inductance changes
Inductance value increased = curve moves to the left
Inductance value decreased = curve moves to the right

In parallel circuits the voltages of parallel branches are equal. That means that the higher voltage drop of the inductor (for higher frequencies) will mean there is also a higher voltage for the load at higher frequencies.

Even though the frequency response curves are the opposite from what they were with an inductor in series, the same thing happens if the inductance value is increased or decreased.

The frequency response curve will move to the left for an increase in value (Figure 5-2), or to the right for a decrease in value (Figure 5-3).
