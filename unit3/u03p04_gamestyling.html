If we place an inductor in series between an audio source and the load, as shown in figure 4-1, we create a low pass filter. Low pass filters are also known as high cut filters. They pass the low frequencies and cut the high frequencies.

In other words, low frequencies get through easier than high frequencies.

[For these examples we will use a speaker as the load in our schematics.]

To represent frequency response we will use a graph that has a vertical (y) axis that represents output voltage (or volume) and a horizontal (x) axis that represents frequency from low (left) to high (right). Figure 4-2 shows the frequency response drawn for the low pass filter created by an inductor in series. The buttons below figure 4-1 allow you to listen to the "flat" audio (original audio with no filter) and the audio through the high pass filter.

If we increase the value of the inductance, either by adding more inductors in series or swapping for a larger inductor, the graph for frequency response will move to the left as shown in Figure 4-2. A simple way to remember this is that toward the left of the graph are the lower frequencies which are larger in wavelength. This image also allows you to listen to the original high pass filter and how it changes with a change in inductance.

Larger inductance = Lower Frequencies.

When inductance changes:
Inductance value increased = curve moves to the left. Inductance value decreased = curve moves to the right.

If we decrease the value of the inductance, either by adding inductors in parallel with the first inductor, or using a smaller inductor, the graph for the frequency response will move to the right as shown in figure 4-3. Again the higher frequencies have a smaller wavelength so making the inductance smaller will move the curve to the right, toward the smaller (higher) frequencies. This image also allows you to listen to the original high pass filter and how it changes with a change in inductance.

This idea that the frequency response curve will move to the left for an increase in value (of inductance in this case), or to the right for a decrease in value, ends up being very useful. It works for Inductors in series of parallel and it also works for capacitors which we will discuss in Unit 4.

Basic inductor rules:

1) Inductors allow lower frequencies to pass easier than high frequencies.
or The higher the frequency, the larger the reactance (XL ) of an inductor. (larger XL = larger V drop)

2) The larger the inductance/the larger the wavelength (lower the Frequency) that will be blocked
