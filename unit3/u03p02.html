Resistors have resistance. That resistance is the same for any frequency and even for both AC and DC.
For DC an inductor is simply a piece of wire and only useful in creating a concentrated magnetic field.

If we run an AC signal through an inductor we will get a magnetic field that varies in polarity with the polarity of the AC signal. Remember though that it also lags behind the electrical current. When the positive half of the AC waveform flows through the inductor, a magnetic field is created. For our example, letâ€™s say it has a positive polarity. That positive polarity magnetic field is still hanging around when the electrical signal moves to the negative half of the waveform. The positive polarity magnetic field will try to create current in the inductor going the opposite wave of the, now negative, AC signal. When this happens the two forces cancel each other out creating less current flow. The faster the changes try to happen the more this cancelation will occur.

An AC signal that changes quickly is called high frequency and it is more difficult for high frequencies to move through an inductor than it is for low frequencies.

We can think of the inductor as having a value that is the equivalent of resistance but varies with frequency. The term we use for this is reactance. In the case of an inductor we call it inductive reactance and designate it with the symbol $X_L$. Because we have already used I for current we use $L$ for inductance. Reactance is measured in Ohms ($\Omega$).

Inductive Reactance:
$ X_L = 2 \pi fL $ $f$ = frequency (in hertz) $L$ = inductance (in henries)

The formula for inductive reactance is $ X_L = 2 \pi fL $.

What it means

A high frequency ($f$) will mean that there is a high reactance (as if there were a larger resistor) while a low frequency creates a low reactance (like a smaller resistor). This formula allows you to find the reactance of an inductor. Just as important is the relationship, which is shown in Figure 2-1, and listed below.

The inductive reactance relationship:

  • When frequency increases, reactance increases

  • When inductance increases, reactance increases

  • When frequency decreases, reactance decreases

  • When inductance decreases, reactance decreases
