If we connect capacitors and inductors together both in series with the load, the inductor will create a low pass filter and the capacitor will create a high pass filter. How they interact will depend on whether the capacitor and inductor are connected in series or in parallel.

Band pass filter

Flat

If we connect the capacitor in series and inductor in series with each other as well as in series with the load (as shown in Figure 10-1) we get a band pass filter with the frequency response shown in Figure 10-1. If you remember we were also able to create that filter with 2 inductors: one in series and one in parallel with the load. It wasn’t discussed but you could also create one with two capacitors: one in series and one in parallel with the load. This filter is a first order filter as there is only one reactive component creating the high pass and one creating the low pass. That means the slope on this circuit, for both the high pass and the low pass, will be $ \frac {6dB}{8_{VA}}$.

Let’s analyze what is happening in this circuit. One way that works well with audio circuits is to think about what will happen to high, midrange, and low frequencies as they flow through the circuit.

  1. First they reach the capacitor

    • Low frequencies: They get to the capacitor, see a high reactance and much of their voltage is dropped by the capacitor meaning that little gets to the speaker.

    • Midrange frequencies: They see a reactance that is not as high as the low frequencies see, but not as low as the high frequencies. Some of their voltage is lost but not as much as with the low frequencies.

    • High frequencies: They see a low reactance so most of their voltage makes it to the speaker.

  2. Then they reach the inductor

    • Low frequencies: They were gone after the capacitor so, even though they see a low reactance through the inductor, there is not much of them left to pass.

    • Midrange frequencies: They see a reactance that is not as high as the low frequencies see, but not as low as the high frequencies. Some of their voltage is lost but not as much as with the high frequencies.

    • High frequencies: They will see a high reactance so a lot of their voltage will be dropped.

All of the frequencies lose some voltage through the filter, but less for the midrange. That "band" of midrange frequencies is "passed" making this a band pass filter.

If we keep the inductor and capacitor in series with the load but now connect them in parallel with each other as shown in Figure 10-2 we get a band cut filter with the frequency response shown in Figure 10-2.

Once again we will analyze the circuit by looking at what happens as frequencies pass through. In this instance however, the capacitor and inductor are reached at the same time and the audio has a choice to go through the inductor or capacitor. The easier path (lower reactance) will be chosen.

  • Low frequencies: They will see a high reactance through the capacitor and a low reactance through the inductor. Flowing through the inductor very little of their voltage will be lost because of the low reactance of the inductor.

  • High frequencies: They will see a high reactance through the inductor and a low reactance through the capacitor. Flowing through the capacitor very little of their voltage will be lost because of the low reactance of the capacitor.

  • Midrange frequencies: They will see a medium value reactance through either the capacitor or inductor. In either case some of their voltage will be lost.

That leaves us with the frequency response shown in Figure 10-2, a band cut filter.
