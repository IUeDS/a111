Capacitors, like inductors, affect different frequencies in different ways. This makes them another reactive component

Capacitors are the inverse of inductors, they have a lower reactance for higher frequencies and a larger reactance for lower frequencies. Some schematic symbols for capacitors are shown in Figure 1-1. The two images in Figure 1-1 are simply different ways of drawing capacitors, there is no meaning in the differences. Figure 1-2 shows a number of different capacitors.

A capacitor is an electrical device which has capacitance. Capacitance is defined as the property of a capacitor which opposes any change in voltage across the capacitor. Capacitance is measured in units called farads and is represented by the symbol $F$. Most capacitors used are less than one farad. Their values are most commonly given as microfarad $(µF)$ or picofarad $(pF)$. A microfarad is 10-6 of a farad and a picofarad is 10-12 of a farad. You may also see nanofarads $(nF)$ which is 10-9 of a farad.

How They Work

A capacitor is made up of two conductive plates with a space in between. The space is filled with an insulator. This insulator is called the dielectric. Figure 1-3 shows the physical construction of a capacitor.

Three things determine the capacitance of the capacitor:

  • surface area of the plates

  • spacing of the plates

  • the dielectric material

Polarized and Non-Polarized Capacitors

Capacitors can be identified as either polarized or non-polarized. When a capacitor is polarized one end needs to be connected to the more positive part of the circuit and the other end to the more negative. If a capacitor is non-polarized it means that the two ends of the capacitor are interchangeable. Non-polarized (N.P.) or Bipolar (B.P.) capacitors can be identified by their schematic symbols. Figure 1-4 shows schematic symbols for both polarized and non-polarized capacitors.

Dielectric Types

Most capacitors are named after their dielectric material and/or construction (electrolytic, ceramic, film and foil). Many also describe the conductive plate material in their name. For instance, a "polystyrene film and foil" capacitor uses metal foil for the plates and a polystyrene film for the dielectric.

Electrolytic capacitors are very common, especially for higher values (over $1µF$) of capacitance. Some examples of electrolytic capacitors are shown in Figure 1-5. With an electrolytic capacitor there is a single plate of metal film and a material (cloth or paper) soaked in an electrolytic solution that acts as both the second plate and the insulator.

Capacitors commonly come in two different physical formats, axial and radial. These are shown in Figure 1-6.

  • Radial capacitors: The two leads both come out of the same side of the capacitor. The longer of the two leads is the positive connection (if it is polarized). The negative lead is also indicated with a colored stripe containing the "-" symbol on the body of the capacitor.

  • Axial Capacitors: The two leads come out of opposite ends of the capacitor. The positive lead is indicated by a groove in the end of the body of the capacitor.

A list of some common capacitor types as well as uses are shown in the table below and Figure 1-7 shows some examples. Note that this is intended as an overview with the value ranges being rough and the uses subjective.

Type Available Values Polarized? Uses
Electrolytic $1µF – 100,000µf$ Both* DC coupling, power supply filtering
Ceramic $1pF – 100,000pF$ Non-polarized High voltage circuits, RF filtering, inexpensive
Tantalum $0.1µF – 330µF$ Polarized DC coupling, used in some esteemed vintage audio circuits
Polystyrene film and foil $150pF – 0.01µF$ Non-polarized DC coupling, best for audio though can be difficult to find
Polypropylene film and foil $100pF – 4.7µF$ Non-polarized DC coupling, good for audio, inexpensive
Mylar film and foil $0.001µF – 4.7µF$ Non-polarized DC coupling, OK for audio, inexpensive

* Non-polarized electrolytic capacitors are actually constructed from two polarized capacitors connected in parallel inside a single case.

Values and Prefixes

The value of a capacitor is measured in Farads. In most analog electronics typical values of capacitors range from 0.00000000001 Farads up to almost 1 Farad. It is actually rare to see a capacitor with a value as high as 1 Farad. Below are some common prefixes used for capacitors and their mathematical relationships.

Commonly used for capacitors:

  • 1 Farad = $1F$ (this is rarely seen)

  • 1 Microfarad = $1µF$ = 10-6 Farads

  • 1 NanoFarad = $1nF$ = 10-9 Farads

  • 1 PicoFarad = $1pF$ = 10-12 Farads
*note that $mF$ $(10^{-3}F)$ is not commonly used

Some other useful conversions:

  • $1µF = 1,000nF = 1,000,000pF$

  • $1nF = 1,000pF = 0.001µF$

  • $1pF = 0.000001µF = 0.001nF$
