When we pass current through a resistor and it drops voltage, that voltage is given off as heat. The energy has to be used and the resistor gives it off as heat. In most cases, we don't want this heat. It is an unintentional side effect.

When a capacitor drops voltage, however, it is actually controlling the current flowing through the circuit. This means there is no energy wasted as heat.

For a rough analogy, imagine a cereal dispenser like the one shown in Figure 5-1.

Imagine you have a friend "Resistor" with their mouth under the dispenser and cereal pouring out. What they can't fit in their mouth goes spilling to the floor.

Your other friend "Capacitor" also has their mouth under another dispenser. Instead of all the cereal that won't fit into their mouth pouring onto the floor, they only let as much out of the dispenser as will fit into their mouth. None ends up on the floor.

The cereal on the floor (from the resistor) is the energy given off as heat. Capacitors don't waste that energy, instead they simply don't allow it to flow. Because of this you will see capacitors used in ceiling fans to change the speed. The speed of the fan is controlled by the voltage. Higher voltage = faster speed. when you pull on the chain to change the speed of a ceiling fan, you are adding a capacitor in series (for middle speed) and a larger capacitor in series (for slow speed) or no capacitor (for fast speed).
