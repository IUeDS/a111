The processes that are at work inside a capacitor are much more complex than those in an inductor. An overview of the basic concepts, however, will help you gain an understanding of how capacitors function in circuits.

The best analogy for a capacitor I have found is a newton’s cradle and is demonstrated in the accompanying video. The center ball of the newton’s cradle represents the plates of the capacitor. The other balls represent the electrons moving in the circuit. The electrons hit one side of the capacitor (let’s say the left side) and push the electrons off of the other (right) side. After that nothing can happen until electrons push back against the right side of the capacitor. This cycle has to keep repeating, electrons moving one direction and then the other. That’s a pretty good description of AC current which we learned about in Chapter 1 isn’t it?

Notice that the "electrons" in the analogy don't ever move from one side of the "capacitor" (center ball) to the other. This is not a deficiency in the analogy, this is what happens in a capacitor. Electrons do not flow through a capacitor. We can think about AC current moving through a capacitor, as it can make understanding easier, but in reality current does not flow through a capacitor. The electrons hit one side and that causes the electrons on the other side of the capacitor to move away from the capacitor into the circuit.

DC current only moves in one direction though, so it can’t move the electrons back onto the capacitor plates. However, DC can create that initial movement of electrons. When you first turn DC on with a capacitor in the circuit the voltage goes from 0 to some voltage. That is a change that makes the capacitor move electrons from one of its plates into the circuit. This is why (particularly on older equipment) you can hear a noise when you first turn on a piece of audio equipment.

Charge and Dis-charge


View video in a new tab (option for full screen)

For capacitors to behave in the way discussed so far they need to be charged with a voltage. This happens very quickly for most common values of capacitors but it is still necessary to understand the process. Once the capacitor plates have been charged, that charge stays in the capacitor for a while, until it discharges. This charge/dis-charge concept is well represented in the newton’s cradle analogy used earlier. The silver balls on the outside of the cradle represent electrons, and the first step in making them move is to pull them away from the black "capacitor" ball. As you learned in chapter 1, moving electrons requires a voltage. That energy (whether voltage or the force of the balls falling back toward the cradle) will last for some length of time and then the balls or electrons will stop moving. Pulling back the silver balls represents the charging of the capacitor and the amount of time they continue moving after that represents the disc-charging of the capacitor.

How long this charging and dis-charging take are directly related to the capacitance. A small value capacitor will charge and dis-charge much faster than a larger value capacitor. If you made an extremely large value capacitor you would end up with a battery. In that case it takes a long time to charge and (we hope) a long time to dis-charge.
