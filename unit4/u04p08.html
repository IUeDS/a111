Just as inductors had RL filters, capacitors have RC filters as shown in Figure 8-1.


As with the inductor formula, the cutoff frequency is the point at which the signal has changed by 3dB. The formula for cutoff frequency in a passive first order capacitor filter is $f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$. This same formula is used for either high pass or low pass. Resistance must be in Ohms and capacitance in Farads, using $k\Omega$ and/or $µF$ will not work.

Passive Capacitor Cutoff Frequency
$f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$

Now let’s work through finding the cutoff frequency. For this example, we will set R1 to $159\Omega$ and C1 to $1µF$.



Figure 8-1: Cutoff Frequency Solution
Step 1: Choose the correct formula. $f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$
Step 2: Convert $µF$ $1µF = 0.000001F$
Step 3: Enter known values. $f_C = \frac{1}{2 \times \pi \times 159 \times 0.000001}$
Step 4: Solve the denominator. $ f_C = \frac{1}{0.000999} $
Step 5: Solve it! $ f_C = 1,000Hz $

Now let’s see what happens if we increase the capacitance. We know that it should shift the curve to the left, making the cutoff frequency lower. Let’s see if the math agrees. We will increase the capacitance by 10x to $10µF$ $(0.00001F)$.

Increased Capacitance Cutoff Frequency Solution
Step 1: Choose the correct formula. $f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$
Step 2: Convert $µF$ $10µF = 0.00001F$
Step 3: Enter known values. $f_C = \frac{1}{2 \times \pi \times 159 \times 0.00001}$
Step 4: Solve the denominator. $ f_C = \frac{1}{0.00999} $
Step 5: Solve it! $ f_C = 100Hz $

Nice to see that the math agrees! Now let’s try reducing the capacitance from the original $1µF$ to $0.1µF$. Since we are reducing the capacitance the curve should move to the right making the cutoff frequency higher.

Reduced Capacitance Cutoff Frequency Solution
Step 1: Choose the correct formula. $f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$
Step 2: Convert $µF$ $0.1µF = 0.0000001F$
Step 3: Enter known values. $f_C = \frac{1}{2 \times \pi \times 159 \times 0.0000001}$
Step 4: Solve the denominator. $ f_C = \frac{1}{0.0000999} $
Step 5: Solve it! $ f_C = 10,010Hz $

To review, our cutoff frequencies(rounded) are:

  • $0.1µF$: $f_C=10kHz$

  • $1µ$F: $f_C=1kHz$

  • $10µF$: $f_C=100Hz$

Relationships

Notice that $f_C$ and capacitance have an inverse relationship, as shown in Figure 8-3. As capacitance is increased, $f_C$ is decreased. This is the same inverse relationship between capacitance value and $f_C$ that we saw between inductance and $f_C$ in Chapter 3.

Change that Resistor

As with inductor RL filters, we can change the resistor in an RC filter to change $f_C$. With inductors, resistance had a direct relationship with $f_C$, as $R$ increased so did $f_C$. For capacitors resistance has an inverse relationship with $f_C$, as shown in Figure 8-4. Mathematically this is because $R$ is on the bottom of the equation for RC filters while it was on the top for RL filters.

Now let’s try decreasing the value of our $159\Omega$ resistor to $15.9\Omega$ in our original circuit that had a $1µF$ capacitor. Since we are decreasing the resistance we should see $f_C$ increase. Originally with the $159\Omega$ resistor $f_C$ was $1kHz$. We are decreasing $R$ by 10x so we should see a 10x increase in $f_C$, which should make it $10kHz$ ($10.01kHz$ before rounding).

Decreased Resistance Cutoff Frequency Solution
Step 1: Choose the correct formula. $f_C = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$
Step 2: Convert $µF$ $1µF = 0.000001F$
Step 3: Enter known values. $f_C = \frac{1}{2 \times \pi \times 15.9 \times 0.000001}$
SStep 4: olve the denominator. $ f_C = \frac{1}{0.00999} $
Step 5: Invert to solve it! $ f_C = 10,010Hz $

As with inductors it is much easier to change resistance than capacitance, so this can be very valuable.
