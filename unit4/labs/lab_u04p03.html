Question #16: What will happen if we add a capacitor in series with the speaker while keeping the inductor in parallel with the speaker?

Low frequencies will not be as loud
Correct! In this case we are adding two different High Pass filters together. Each on its own has a 6dB per octave slope. when we add them together we get a 12dB per octave slope. The lows are cut more drastically with the high frequencies affected less.
Low frequencies will be louder
That is not correct. In this case we are adding two different High Pass filters together. Each on its own has a 6dB per octave slope. when we add them together we get a 12dB per octave slope. The lows are cut more drastically with the high frequencies affected less.
High frequencies will not be as loud
That is not correct. In this case we are adding two different High Pass filters together. Each on its own has a 6dB per octave slope. when we add them together we get a 12dB per octave slope. The lows are cut more drastically with the high frequencies affected less.
High frequencies will be louder
There may indeed be some increase in the lower high frequencies, but what will really change is that the lows will be cut more, while the highs will be affected less (even though they were already only affected a tiny amount).

Question #18: For our next experiment we will connect a capacitor and an inductor both in series with the speaker and in series with each other. What kind of filter will this create?

Nope. All audio has to pass through both the capacitor and the inductor.
That means that ALL audio will be cut some.
  • Low frequencies will be cut a lot by the capacitor
  • High frequencies will be cut a lot by the inductor
  • Midrange frequencies will be cut a little by both filters, but less overall than either high or low frequencies.
This leaves us with a Band Pass Filter.
