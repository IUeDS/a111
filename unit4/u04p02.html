Capacitive reactance is the capacitor’s equivalent of the resistor’s resistance. The difference is that resistance for a resistor is always the same, no matter what the frequency of the signal or whether it is AC or DC. Reactance, however, changes with frequency. Reactance is represented by the letter $X$ and is measured in Ohms.

A capacitor is the inverse of an inductor. The reactance of a capacitor decreases with frequency. Let's look at what that means.

Imagine an AC signal going through a capacitor (the capacitor is in series between the source and load). The higher the frequency of the signal, the lower the reactance and the easier it is for the signal to pass through it. The lower the frequency, the more difficult it is for the signal to pass.

$x_{c} = \frac {1}{2\pi fC} $ $f$ = frequency (in hertz) $C$ = capacitance (in farads)

Capacitors and DC

If you imagine a frequency getting lower and lower, or slower and slower, you can imagine that it would eventually stop entirely. This would be DC, a voltage that does not change over time. Capacitors block DC. Since the reactance of a capacitor increases as frequency decreases, it is greatest for the lowest frequencies. With DC it is so high that no DC can flow through a capacitor. In situations where there is AC and DC together you can think of a capacitor as an open circuit for DC and a resistor (with a value equal to the reactance for the signal frequency) for AC.

In Chapter 1 you investigated AC riding on DC. When we start looking at circuits with active components in Chapter 5 and beyond, you will learn that most active components need to have DC added to AC in order to function. Capacitors are invaluable for separating the DC out so that only the AC is left.

We only want the AC at the output of the circuit. That means we must take the DC away from the AC riding on DC. A capacitor in series will block all of the DC but allow the AC through. If we make the capacitor too small, it will also reduce some of the low frequency AC but with a large enough capacitor all of the AC (audio) can pass through.
