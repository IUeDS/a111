What are some other ways to describe AC waveforms besides voltage?

Frequency (f): An AC waveform moves from a positive peak to a negative peak once during each cycle. How many times it does this per second is the frequency. The frequency is the inverse of the period. 1/period = frequency and 1/frequency = period. 1/period = frequency and 1/frequency = period. Frequency is defined in hertz (Hz).

Period (T): The time it takes an AC waveform to complete a single cycle. Period is the inverse of frequency. 1/period = frequency and 1/frequency = period. Period is defined in seconds.

Wavelength (Λ or Lambda): Waveforms can also be described by their wavelength. Wavelength is a description of how far a wave moves during a cycle. If we are talking about a sound wave in air, the formula for wavelength is: wavelength = speed of sound/frequencyÂ or Λ = s/f.

Speed of Sound: Sound moves at (roughly) 1,126 feet /second. that means a 1000Hz wave would have a wavelength of: Λ = 1,126/1000 or 1.126ft.

AC comes in other waveforms as well. The two most common after the sine wave are the square wave and the triangle wave. All digital signals are square waves so they are incredibly common in modern electronics.

Square wave:


In figure 15-1 you will see a display of a square wave going through one cycle. Most square waves used for digital electronics do not actually have current flowing in both directions. They move between no current flow for a time (0 volts) and a peak positive voltage. In that case it could actually be defined as DC because current only flows in one direction. Square waves can also move from a positive peak to a negative peak, in which case they are true AC because current reverses directions.

A true square wave would move instantly from the positive peak to the negative peak (or 0 volts) but in reality it takes some time to change. This makes square waves useful for testing electronic devices as it demonstrates how quickly current can change within a device.





Triangle wave:


In figure 15-2 you will see a display of a triangle wave going through one cycle. A triangle wave is similar to a sine wave but instead of moving from 0 volts to a positive peak in a curve it moves in a straight line. This is a more complex tone and can also be described as having more harmonics. Beyond synthesizers, triangle waves are rarely used.
