In order to design and understand electronic circuits without actually building them, and even as an aid in building them, we use schematics. A schematic is a visual representation of the electronic circuit. Figure 5-1 shows the schematic for a Fender Champ guitar amplifier. You can think of a schematic as a map.

That map shows:

  • the starting point (our Voltage source)

  • all possible roads (the connections or wires)

  • the places you can stop (the components we will be learning about)

The symbols we will use often contain a great deal of information so be sure to note all the details in the schematic symbol. Some starting symbols are shown below and more will be given with each new component we discuss. We are starting with just the basics of how and why electrons move; later sections will present a new electrical concept, an associated component, and related circuits.

All of the components within a schematic are connected with "wires" which are just lines drawn on the schematic. These are paths which allow the electrons to flow from one component to the next, and then back to where they started.

DC Sources

We now see two different ways of representing DC voltage sources as schematic symbols (Figure 5-2 and 5-3). Yes, this is the first you have heard voltage described as DC, more on that will come later. The other kind of voltage and current we will discuss is AC and the symbols look a little different, we will look at those later in this chapter.

The DC Voltage source symbols in Figure 5-2 are the most generic versions. They simply indicate which end is positive and which is negative. Whether the "V" is in the symbol or not does not change the meaning of the symbol. The symbols in Figure 5-3 were originally used only for batteries, with the number of double lines indicating the number of "cells" that made up the battery. In modern use they can represent any DC voltage source, not just a battery. The longer line (horizontal in Figure 5-3) always indicates the positive end of the voltage source and the shorter line the negative. The + symbol may or may not be there. Whether it is or not does not change the meaning of the symbol.
