There are two basic ways to connect components in a circuit.

Series Circuits

If two components are connected in series, an electron flowing through one component must flow through the other component.

If there is any way for the electron to flow through one component and not the other, then the two components cannot be said to be connected in series.

In our circuit with the voltage source, switch, and light bulb, all three of those were in series with each other. Any electron going through one of those components or the voltage source would have to go through all of them. Figure 9-1 shows two light bulbs in series with each other. In our circuit with the voltage source, switch, and light bulb, all three of those were in series with each other. There was no way an electron going through one of those components or the voltage source would not go through all of them. Figure 9-1 shows two light bulbs in series with each other. Parallel Circuits

If an electron at a point in a circuit can move through either of two components to end up at the same new point in the circuit, then the components are said to be in parallel.

If the electron must start or end in a different place to go through either component, then they cannot be said to be in parallel.

Figure 9-2 shows two light bulbs connected in parallel. An electron at either end of the two light bulbs could choose to go through either of the light bulbs and end up in the same place. Each light bulb is said to be in a different branch of the circuit.
