What is the relationship between voltage sources and loads in a circuit?

Voltage sources provide the voltage that moves the electrons while the load uses up that voltage. Typically, there is just a single voltage source but there can be more than one load. A load is said to drop the voltage that it uses. The load(s) and voltage source have a very simple relationship: the total voltage of the source must equal the total of the voltage drops. This relationship is described in the second of Kirchoff’s circuit laws if you would like to investigate it in more detail.

So far we have only discussed voltage sources. There are, in actuality, two types of sources, voltage sources and current sources. For this text we will just focus on the most common, which is the voltage source.

A voltage source will be defined at a particular voltage. A 9V battery, for instance, is a voltage source that puts out 9 volts. How much current this creates is dependent upon the load. Let’s look at the circuit shown in Figure 8-1 for example, where there is a 9W light bulb. If we connect that to our 9V battery, we know that the voltage source (9V) must equal the voltage drops. Since the light bulb makes up the entire load it must drop all 9V.

So, how much current is flowing in the circuit shown in figure 8-1?

${P} = {V}*{I} $
so
$ {9W} = {9V} \times {?A} $
so
$ {I} = {1A} $

If we used an 18W light bulb instead then there would be 2A of current. The load, which in this case is the light bulb, determines how much current is flowing. Remember that the load could be something as simple as a single light bulb or an incredibly complex circuit. In extremes, and all cases in between (with a voltage source), the load determines how much current will flow from the source into the load.
