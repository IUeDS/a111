Elements in their normal state contain the same number of protons and electrons. In an introductory view of the atom, the electrons revolve around the nucleus in orbits, like planets around the sun. The currently accepted atomic structure is much more complicated, but the "orbit" concept, known as the "Bohr Model," and shown in Figure 2-1, will still work for the purposes of understanding electronics. For most elements the outer orbit does not contain the maximum number of electrons, but all of the more inner orbits do.

Electrons in the outer orbit are called valence electrons. The fewer valence electrons, the more likely the atom is to have (and accept) free electrons, which means it will conduct electricity.

A conductor is an element with less than half the maximum number of valence electrons. An insulator is an element with more than half of the maximum number of valence electrons. The fewer valence electrons in an atom, the better the conductor and the easier it is to get the electrons to move.

Let's take a conductor and place a more positive charge at one end and a more negative charge at the other. Imagine that the glass tube shown in Figure 2-2 is a conductor with a negative charge on the left and a positive charge on the right. What will happen to the smiley face electrons?
