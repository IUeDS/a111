There are two basic types of current: direct current (DC) and alternating current (AC). Direct current (the kind of current that we have been discussing so far) has electrons that move in the same direction all the time. Alternating current has electrons moving in one direction, then the opposite direction and repeating at some frequency.

But don’t electrons always move from more negative to more positive?
Yes, but with alternating current, the charges that cause the movement of electrons change.

For instance, let’s hook one end of a conductor to ground and the other end to a charge of +10 volts. Electrons are going to move from ground to the +10 volts. Now let’s switch the +10 volts for –10 volts. Now the electrons will move the opposite direction. If we keep switching the + and – voltages we will have an alternating current. Alternating current is constantly changing. The pattern of the signal is called the waveform. The most common waveform is the sine wave.

Sine wave:

In Figure 14-1 you will see a display of a sine wave going through one cycle. The up/down axis represents voltage, while the left/right axis represents time. The sine wave is the simplest of all AC signals and is also referred to as a "pure" tone, because it contains no harmonics. More on harmonics to come. The sine wave is also the AC waveform we will utilize most often in this text.

We will use sine waves to represent generic AC waveforms throughout this text and in your labs.

The sine wave is considered the most important and fundamental of all AC waveforms. This is because any other waveform can be created by adding together sine waves of different frequencies and amplitudes. The sine wave is important to electronics engineers because of its purity. It consists of only a single frequency, and it alternates regularly above and below its mid value at a constant rate. In musical terms this rate of repeat, the frequency, would be called "pitch". While other waveforms contain multiple frequencies, the sine wave only contains a single frequency, called the fundamental. The other frequencies in a more complex waveform are called harmonics and account for the difference in "sound" of different waveforms.

Ways to define
AC Voltages

Vrms = 0.707 x Vpk

Direct current signals tend to stay the same voltage over time; therefore we can describe them with a simple voltage. AC voltages, however, change in a cyclical way. A sine wave, for example, starts at 0 volts then goes up to its maximum positive voltage, back to 0, down to its maximum negative voltage, then back to 0. Since the voltage is constantly changing it must be defined in one of three ways.

  • Peak voltage (Vpk) is the maximum (peak) voltage that the AC signal attains.

  • Peak to Peak (Vpk-pk) is the voltage measured from the maximum (peak) positive voltage to the maximum (peak) negative voltage. With most AC signals the numeric value of the positive and negative peaks are the same, only the polarity is different, so the Vpk-pk is typically twice the Vpk. The most useful way to describe the potential of an AC signal is to use Vrms.

  • Vrms: RMS (root mean square) is a statistical measure of the magnitude of a varying quantity. Vrms measures AC signals in a way that gives a voltage value that is equal to what the voltage would be if it were converted to DC. Most VOMs (volt-ohm meters) will read AC signals in Vrms. To convert to Vrms multiply Vpk by 0.707. To convert back to Vpk multiply Vrms by 1.414. This is a simplification of the formula that will work for our purposes. The actual formula is $ V_{rms} = \frac {V_{PK}} {\sqrt2} $.
