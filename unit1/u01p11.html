Voltage is the difference in potential between two points. This means that when measuring voltage, a meter must be connected in parallel. A voltmeter has two connections: a positive connection (red in a real world meter) and a negative connection (black in a real world meter). The voltmeter will display a value for the difference in voltage between those two connections. Remember that voltages in parallel branches of a circuit are the same, so the voltmeter is going to have the same voltage across it as the light bulb. This is shown in Figure 11-1 with the voltmeter measuring across the first light bulb.

Ideally the voltmeter acts as an observer that does not affect the circuit. It just senses the amount of voltage without changing anything about the circuit.

As discussed on page 3, there are two ways to think about voltage measurements. If we say "the voltage dropped by the light bulb is 6V," then we have measured as shown in figure 11-1. We could also say "the voltage across the light bulb is 6V."

If we say "the voltage at the left side of the first light bulb is 12V," then we are measuring from that point to ground as shown in Figure 11-2. That is often also described as "voltage with respect to ground." In most cases it will simply be said that the voltage is something at a point. In that case it is understood that it is being compared to ground
