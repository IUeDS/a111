We have discussed two ways of describing electricity so far: current and voltage. Another way of describing electricity is power. Power is defined as the rate at which work is done. Power is measured in units called watts and is represented by P. The formula for power is simply voltage multiplied by current: P=VI. For the most part we will only use power when describing the limits of some components (like resistors, which will be covered in Chapter 2) or the output of some amplifiers.

The devices that we place in an electronic circuit are called components. Some of the components we will be learning about are: resistors, inductors, transformers, and capacitors. In order to make our first simple circuit, we need something to use up the electricity. For now, we will be using a simple light bulb in our circuits. The component or components that are using the electricity are referred to as the load. The load could be as simple as the light bulb that we will use in our first circuit or it could be an incredibly complex circuit.

Two schematic symbols for light bulbs are shown in figure 7-1. Light bulbs can also be called lamps. The different symbols indicate a difference in how the light bulb is constructed, but we will ignore that for the purposes of this text. What is important is that when you pass a current through a light bulb it lights up.

You must get eight in a row correct to move to the next phase. Each progressive phase will give you fewer visual cues. You must complete all four phases in order to get credit for the game.
