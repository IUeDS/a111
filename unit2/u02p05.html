If you have some number, say 3, of the same resistor value in parallel you simply divide the value of that resistor value by 3 (or however many resistor there are in parallel) to get the total resistance. An example is shown in Figure 5-1 with three $33K\Omega$ resistor. The total resistance is $\frac{33K\Omega}{3}$ or $11K\Omega$. This is simply a shortcut for the inverse rule. $\frac{1}{33K\Omega} + \frac{1}{33K\Omega} + \frac{1}{33K\Omega} = \frac{1}{R_{total}}$

This means $R_{total} = \frac{33K\Omega}{3} = 11K\Omega$

You can also divide a large parallel circuit into more easily manageable sets of parallel resistors. In Figure 5-2 you see a circuit of parallel resistors. We can use the long formula to find the total resistance.

$\frac{1}{22K\Omega} + \frac{1}{22K\Omega} + \frac{1}{33K\Omega} + \frac{1}{33K\Omega} + \frac{1}{33K\Omega} = \frac{1}{R_{total}}$

I’m not sure about you, but I can’t do that without a calculator. However, by dividing the circuit into two sub-circuits I can find the total without a calculator. Since we can easily find the total when the resistor values are the same we will divide the circuit into two sub-circuits. One sub-circuit with $22K\Omega$ resistors and a second sub-circuit with three $33K\Omega$ resistors and solve for each of those individually, simplifying each to a single resistor. Then you combine those two "sub-circuit" resistors as shown in Figure 5-3.

  • We find the total for the two $22K\Omega$ resistors in parallel $(\frac {22K\Omega}{2} = 11K\Omega)$.

  • Then find the total of the three $33K\Omega$ resistors in parallel $(\frac{33K\Omega}{3} = 11K\Omega)$

  • We can then represent those sub-circuits as two single $11K\Omega$ resistors in parallel with each other. These are shown in Figure 5-3 as "R1+2" and "R3+4+5"

In this case the result is then easy to find $(\frac{11K\Omega}{2} = 5.5K\Omega)$. Even if the two values were not the same it would still be simpler to find the total than it was with five values to add. This is a useful strategy particularly in more complex situations.
