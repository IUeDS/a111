In Chapter 1 we talked about conductors and insulators. With an insulator, it is very difficult for electrons to move. With the best conductors, it is very easy for electrons to move. Not all conductors are the same, however, some allow electron movement more easily than others.

All conductors have some resistance to them. Most of the time in this text and in electronics in general we will connect components in our theoretical designs with wires that are "perfect" conductors. These have no resistance but donâ€™t exist in the real world. For the most part the addition of "real" conductors when the designs are built does not affect the way the circuit functions.

We can think of this from the opposite perspective and look at how well something resists the flow of electrons. This property of resisting the flow of electrons is called resistance. With a large resistance, it is more difficult for current to flow while it is easier for current to flow with a smaller resistance.

Resistance is measured in units called ohms and represented with the letter $R$. The symbol for ohms is $\Omega$ (omega).
