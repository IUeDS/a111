There is a special type of resistor used a great deal in audio called a potentiometer. Potentiometers have a post that the user can turn and affect the electronic circuit. The most common example would be a volume control. It is common to refer to a potentiometer as a "pot". Various potentiometers are shown in Figure 9-1.

The schematic symbol for a potentiometer is shown in Figure 9-2.

Volume controls (at least in analog electronics) are potentiometers. Typically turning the control down (counter-clockwise) decreases the volume, while turning it up (clockwise) increases the volume.

A potentiometer (pot) has 3 connections: (only the wiper is differentiated in the schematic symbol)

  • Wiper - connection that moves and increases resistance to one of the other connections while reducing resistance to the other.

  • CW (clockwise) - connection that has a decreased resistance to the wiper as the potentiometer is turned clockwise when looking at top of potentiometer.

  • CCW (counter-clockwise) - connection that has a decreased resistance to the wiper as the potentiometer is turned counter-clockwise when looking at top of pot.

Figure 9-3 shows a potentiometer drawn as two resistors. This is a good way to think of how a potentiometer works. One resistor is from the CW connection to the wiper and the second resistor is from the wiper to the CCW connection. These two resistors have values that always add up to the total value of the potentiometer.

The example in Figure 9-3 shows a $10K\Omega$ potentiometer with the equivalent two resistors next to it. If the potentiometer was turned to the halfway point then the equivalent resistors would both measure $5K\Omega$.

If the potentiometer were turned clockwise then R1 would be reduced in value while R2 is increased. In all situations they would add up to be $10k\Omega$.

When you look at the equivalent circuit of R1 and R2 you should recognize it as a voltage divider. With R1 and R2 at equal values as shown half of the source voltage would get to the wiper as shown in Figure 9-4. This would be the typical way to connect a potentiometer as a volume control.
