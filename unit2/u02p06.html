One of the ways to make complex circuits easier to understand is to divide them up into smaller sub-circuits. The first sub-circuit we will look at is the voltage divider.

When multiple resistors are connected in series with a voltage at one end (either positive or negative, AC or DC) and a different voltage at the other end (often ground) you have a voltage divider.

Let’s take a simple example with four resistors of the same value with $ +40VDC $ at the top and ground (0 volts) at the bottom as shown in Figure 6-1.

  • Since this is a series circuit we know that current is the same through all four resistors.

  • The resistors have the same resistance and the same current, so Ohm’s law tells us that they will have the same voltage drop.

  • We also know that the sum of the voltage drops must equal the voltage source ($40V$ in this case).

The four equal voltage drops across each resistor in Figure 6-1 add up to $40V$ total.

$ \frac {40V}{4} = 10V $

That means that each resistor drops $ 10V $.
$ V_{R1} = 10V, V_{R2} = 10V, V_{R3} = 10V, V_{R4} = 10V $

Answer:
You may think that Figure 6-1 is not a complete circuit. It is, even though there you may not see a complete circuit drawn.

In order to reduce clutter in schematics it is common to not draw the voltage source as having two connections in the same place. Instead the positive (+40V in this case) and the negative (GND in this case) are added as connection points wherever needed. The electrons still start at the negative lead of the voltage source and get back to the positive lead but the voltage source is not shown at a single place on the schematic.

There could even be multiple points around a schematic labeled +40 and GND. They would all be connected to the voltage source. This allows for the reduction of clutter in the schematic.
We can use the shorthand $V_{R1}$ for "voltage drop of $R1$".

In Figure 6-2 you see that a voltmeter connected across each resistor agrees with our math above to show that each resistor is dropping $10V$. Figure 6-1 and 6-2 are very simple versions of voltage dividers with all the resistors having the same value. No matter what the values of the resistors though, there is always a direct relationship between resistance and voltage drop in a series circuit. We know that by putting together Ohm’s law and the fact that current is the same for all resistors in a series circuit.

Remember from Chapter 1 that we can describe the voltage across something, like R2, or we can describe the voltage at a point, such as the connection between R1 and R2. The voltage at that point, between R1 and R2, would be measured compared to GND (at the bottom of R4). We can think of that voltage two ways:

  1. It is $V_{R1}$, or $10V$ toward GND from $40V$ which means $40V - 10V = 30V$

    or

  2. It is $V_{R2} + V_{R3} + V_{R4}$, which we would describe as $30V$ from GND or just $30V$.
