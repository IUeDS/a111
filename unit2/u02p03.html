The most important formula in electronics is Ohm's law: $ {V}={I}{R} $

Ohm’s law shows the relationship between the three main measurements used in a circuit: voltage, current, and resistance. It also shows how the units that are used to measure these relate to each other. 1 volt will cause 1 amp of current to flow through 1 ohm; that same $1V$ will cause $2A$ through $0.5\Omega$. The cartoon in Figure 3-1 illustrates Ohm’s Law very well.

Some other variations:
$ {I}=\frac{V}{R} $, $ {R}=\frac{V}{I} $

If we add the formula for power: $ ({P}={V}{I}) $

We can also have:
$ {P}=\frac{{V}^{2}}{R} $, $ {V}=\frac{P}{I} $, $ {I}=\frac{P}{V} $, $ {R}=\frac{P}{{I}^{2}}$

Let’s plug some numbers into the equation and see what happens.

  • First we'll use some extraordinarily simple numbers: ${1V}={1A} \times {1 \Omega} $

  • Keep the resistance the same and double the voltage: ${2V}={2A} \times {1 \Omega} $

Given any two values in Ohm’s law you can find the third. You can also see the relationship of voltage, current and resistance.

The $V$, $I$, and $R$ in the Ohm’s law equation all must reference the same thing. That can be a single resistor, a combination of resistors, or an entire circuit.

  • If the $I$ and $R$ refer to a single resistor, then $V$ will be the voltage drop of that single resistor.

  • If $R$ refers to the total resistance of two resistors in series, then $I$ would have to refer to the series current through those resistors and $V$ would be the total voltage drop of both resistors in series.

  • If $V$ is the voltage drop of two resistors in parallel, then you could use $I$ for one of the resistors and you could then find $R$ for that resistor. If you used total $I$ for both resistors, then $R$ would be the combined resistance of both resistors.
