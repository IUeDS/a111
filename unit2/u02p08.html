Back in 2-5 we discussed a few tricks for making parallel circuits easier to solve. Now we are going to take that approach to more complex circuits. Let’s take the example circuit in Figure 8-1. This circuit has a combination of series and parallel connections between components.

Step 1:

The first step is to make certain you understand how all the components are connected. In this circuit it is not necessarily obvious as it is drawn. You will often find this to be true as schematics are drawn to fit on the page more often than to be easy to read. To make it easier to understand it is very often a good idea to re-draw a schematic so that it is clear how everything is connected.

Figure 8-2 shows the circuit re-drawn so that it is clearer how the resistors are connected.

  • The series combination of R5 and R6 are in parallel with R7 so how they are drawn was slightly changed to make it look like we are used to seeing parallel components.

  • R4 is in series with R5/R6/R7 so it was turned vertical so it is clearer that it is in series with that sub-circuit of three resistors.

  • R1, R2, and, R3 were left as they were.

In the next steps we will combine resistors in easily combined sub-circuits. It is important that you start this process of simplifying parallel sub-circuits at the opposite side from where you are measuring. If not your results will be wrong.

Step 2:

First R5 and R6 are added to become a $2K\Omega$ resistor to get the simplification shown in Figure 8-3.

Choose the correct formula. $Rsum5, 6 = R5 + R6$
Insert the known values. $Rsum5, 6 = 2K\Omega + 2K\Omega$
Solve it! $ Rsum5, 6 = 4K\Omega $

Step 3:

Next we combine the $2K\Omega$ resistor that is the sum of R5 and R6 from Step 2 with R7. They are both $2K\Omega$ and in parallel so they become a $1k\Omega$ resistor shown in Figure 8-4.

We use the inverse rule to sum R5, R6, and R7 into a resistor we will call $Rsum5,6,7$.

Choose the correct formula. $\frac {1}{Rsum5,6,7} = \frac {1}{Rsum5,6} + \frac {1}{R7}$
Insert the known values. $\frac {1}{2K} + \frac {1}{2K} = \frac {1}{1K}$
Or use the shortcut. $2K\Omega ÷ 2 = 1K\Omega$
Solve it! $Rsum5,6,7 = 1K\Omega$

Step 4:

Next we combine R4 with R5-6-7 as shown in Figure 8-5.

Choose the correct formula. $Rsum4,5,6,7 = Rsum5,6,7 + R4$
Insert the known values. $Rsum4,5,6,7 = 1K\Omega + 1K\Omega$
Solve it! $Rsum4,5,6,7 = 2K\Omega$

Step 5:

Next we combine R1 and R2 into a $4k\Omega$ resistor as shown in Figure 8-6.

Choose the correct formula. $Rsum1,2 = R1 + R2$
Insert the known values. $Rsum1,2 = 2K\Omega + 2K\Omega$
Solve it! $Rsum1,2 = 4K\Omega$

Step 6:

The sum of R1 and R2 can now be easily combined with R3 since they are the same value. That gives us an $Rsum1,2,3$ value of $2k\Omega$ as shown in Figure 8-7.

Choose the correct formula. $\frac {1}{Rsum1,2,3} = \frac {1}{Rsum1,2} + \frac {1}{R3}$
Insert the known values. $\frac {1}{Rsum1,2,3} = \frac {1}{4K\Omega} + \frac {1}{4K\Omega}$
Or use the shortcut. $Rsum1,2,3 = 4K\Omega ÷ 2$
Solve it! $Rsum1,2,3 = 2K\Omega$

Step 7:

We can now easily combine the remaining two resistors, two $2kΩ$ in parallel, into a single $1kΩ$ resistance as shown in Figure 8-8.

Choose the correct formula. $\frac {1}{Rsum1,2,3,4,5,6,7} = \frac {1}{Rsum1,2,3} + \frac {1}{Rsum4,5,6,7}$
Insert the known values. $\frac {1}{Rsum1,2,3,4,5,6,7} = \frac {1}{2K\Omega} + \frac {1}{2K\Omega}$
Or use the shortcut. $Rsum1,2,3,4,5,6,7 = 2k\Omega ÷ 2$
Solve it! $Rsum1,2,3,4,5,6,7 = 1K\Omega$
