The preamplifier section is the first bit of amplification for the signal from the instrument. Most electric guitars will have an output voltage of $100mV_{RMS}$ to $500mV_{RMS}$. The preamplifier stage amplifies this to work within the amplifier.

What Do We Know?

Most tube audio circuits you will find will be based on the circuits we have already studied. The preamplifier circuit, for instance, is a common cathode circuit. Once we define this as a common cathode circuit, we get quite a bit of knowledge that comes along with that. We know, for instance, that the output will be opposite polarity when compared to the input.

In this case we can also identify the purpose of all of the components in this section simply by comparing it to our typical common cathode circuit. The preamplifier section is shown in Figure 5-1.

Notice that there are three components connected directly to the first triode in our circuit. All of these were also seen in our typical common cathode circuit.

  • $1500\Omega$ resistor connected from cathode to ground.

    • Purpose = set $0V$ at grid halfway between saturation and cutoff.

  • $25µF$ capacitor connected in parallel with the $1500\Omega$ resistor.

    • Purpose = provide low reactance path for AC so that cathode voltage remains stable.

  • $100k\Omega$ resistor connected from anode to $+V$.

    • Purpose = convert changing current into changing voltage.

What’s missing?

There is one component from our typical common cathode that is not in the highlighted section of the schematic in Figure 5-1. There should be a capacitor in series with the output to block DC. Although there is not a single capacitor connected as we saw in the typical circuit, we will see that there is no path to the next triode that does not include a series capacitor.
